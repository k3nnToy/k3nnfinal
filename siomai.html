Siomai

Siomai is a steamed dumpling filled with ground pork (sometimes mixed with shrimp or beef), wrapped in a thin flour or wonton wrapper, and typically topped with minced garlic or grated carrots. Served hot and often paired with soy sauce, calamansi, chili garlic oil, or a combination of all three, siomai is juicy, savory, and rich in flavor. Though traditionally Chinese, it has been fully embraced and localized in the Philippines, often sold in food courts, karinderyas, and street stalls.

Cultural Significance

Siomai is a shining example of how the Philippines adapts and “Filipinizes” foreign dishes. Brought over by Chinese immigrants, it quickly found a home in Filipino hearts and stomachs—becoming an everyday snack or meal companion. Affordable, filling, and easy to prepare in bulk, siomai has become a common choice for students, office workers, and merienda-goers. Whether enjoyed on-the-go or as part of a rice meal, siomai represents the multicultural influences that enrich Filipino cuisine while highlighting the country’s love for flavorful, bite-sized comfort food.
